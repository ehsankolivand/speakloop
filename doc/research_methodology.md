# Personalized 12–16 Week English Speaking/Listening Intervention for a Senior Persian-L1 Software Engineer Preparing for Android/Fintech Interviews

## TL;DR
- **The dominant problem is not memory and not grammar knowledge — it is a practice–target mismatch plus incomplete proceduralization.** The learner's declarative knowledge is largely intact (Part B quiz accuracy, correct metalanguage); what is missing is the procedural skill of executing that knowledge under real-time speech-production load. Skill-acquisition theory (DeKeyser & Suzuki, 2025) and Kormos's (2006) speech-production model converge on a single prescription: this skill develops only from output practice under increasing time pressure with feedback, not from more written drills and not from Anki.
- **Reallocate the 60–120 daily minutes to four high-evidence techniques**: (1) shadowing for listening and prosody (Hamada, 2016, *Language Teaching Research* 20(1); Whitworth & Rose, 2025, systematic review of 44 studies), (2) 4/3/2 task-repetition monologues on Android/fintech topics (Nation, 1989; De Jong & Perfetti, 2011, *Language Learning* 61(2), N = 24 adult ESL), (3) dictogloss with self-recasting tied to the learner's own error inventory (Swain & Lapkin, 1995; Lyster & Saito, 2010), and (4) narrow listening on domain-bounded technical content (Krashen, 1996; Rodrigo & Krashen, 2013). Anki is kept only in a redesigned production-cloze form targeting verb+preposition collocations, 3rd-person -s, and multi-word interview chunks.
- **Realistic outcome on a 12–16 week budget**: meaningful gains in utterance fluency (speech rate, mean length of run) and listening comprehension are well documented in this timeline; full proceduralization of 3rd-person -s within 12 weeks is *not*. Pienemann's Processability Theory places -s at the latest morphosyntactic stage and Iranian-EFL replication data (Mohammadkhani, Eslamdoost & Gholamreza'i, 2011, N = 151) confirm this. The protocol therefore optimises for interview-grade *intelligibility and fluency* (Saito & Plonsky, 2019, *Language Learning* 69(3): 652–708; 77 studies, 1982–2017) over native-like grammatical accuracy.

## 1. Diagnosis

The 13-session evidence profile is internally consistent and points to one structural cause with several mechanism-level branches.

### 1.1 Ranked root causes (by impact on 12–16 week interview readiness)

| Rank | Root cause | Mechanism | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Practice–target mismatch** (Pattern 13) | Practice is written, monitored, low-time-pressure; the target is spoken, unmonitored, high-time-pressure. Skill specificity binds gains to the practice modality. | DeKeyser (1997); De Jong (2005); M. Li & DeKeyser (2017): "performance was far worse when participants" were tested in a modality they had not trained. |
| 2 | **Incomplete proceduralization of inflectional morphology under speech load** (Patterns 1, 2, 3, 4, 8) | Declarative rules are present (Part B accuracy, rule-stating). Under speech-production time pressure, attentional resources for grammatical encoding and monitoring are insufficient. | DeKeyser & Suzuki (2025) on skill-acquisition theory; Kormos (2006): "L2 speakers have little attention available for monitoring." |
| 3 | **Processability-stage mismatch for 3rd-singular -s and auxiliary be** (Patterns 1, 2) | -s and aux-be require inter-phrasal feature unification (S-procedure), placed at Stage 5 in Pienemann's hierarchy and the slowest English morpheme to stabilise even with instruction. | Pienemann (1998); Mohammadkhani, Eslamdoost & Gholamreza'i (2011), Procedia 29: 910–916, N = 151 Iranian EFL learners across three proficiency levels. |
| 4 | **Persian L1 transfer for prepositions, articles, and possessive order** (Patterns 5, 7) | Persian's prepositional system, absence of definite article, and post-nominal ezafe possessor map non-isomorphically onto English. Documented as fossilised even at advanced levels. | Jalali & Shojaei (2012), *Reading Matrix* 12(1): 80–97, N = 78, 642 prepositional errors; Joolaee & Ghonsooly (2015), Procedia 192: 438–446. |
| 5 | **Fatigue-driven control collapse in late-evening sessions** (Patterns 6, 8) | Cognitive fatigue narrows working-memory and attention resources, which are precisely what L2 monitoring depends on. Late-evening proceduralisation is fragile. | Heidari (2025), *IRAL*, DOI: 10.1515/iral-2025-0039, N = 92 L2 learners: "spaced practice significantly reduced overall cognitive fatigue, with the effect being most pronounced among low-proficiency learners"; Kormos (1999, 2006). |

### 1.2 The four required diagnostic answers

**(a) Why 3rd-person -s persists despite explicit knowledge.** Pienemann's Processability Theory places 3sg-s at Stage 5, the inter-phrasal S-procedure stage, because the encoder must check the lexical feature [3sg] across the subject NP and the verb. An Iranian-L1 replication (Mohammadkhani, Eslamdoost & Gholamreza'i, 2011, N = 151) explicitly verified that 3sg-s acquisition follows the predicted PT stages: "elementary learners are less developed in their interlanguage and are in lower levels of development based on Processability theory." Persian present-tense verbs do not require this specific inter-phrasal English-style agreement, so the learner cannot rely on L1 transfer here. DeKeyser & Suzuki (2025) frame the persistence as a proceduralisation problem: the rule is *known* but not *compiled*, so it costs attentional resources every time, and any competing demand (lexical retrieval of "Kotlin coroutines," planning the next clause) preempts it. The rule disappears under unscripted load not because it is unknown but because the executive budget is overrun. Amirjalili, Neysani & Nikbakht (2024), *Heliyon* 10(11): e31582, document exactly this in Iranian ESL writers: "Under various task conditions, learners exhibited two opposing patterns of IL variability (increasing and decreasing) in their morphological forms … caused by limited working memory and attention capacity in addition to L1 transfer."

**(b) What the recognition–production asymmetry means mechanistically.** Recognition (Part B) draws on declarative memory and offline judgment; production draws on procedural execution under real-time encoding constraints. DeKeyser (1997) and M. Li & DeKeyser (2017) document this as *skill specificity*: receptive automaticity does not transfer to productive automaticity. Kormos's (2006) model gives the precise architecture: in L2 speech, the conceptualiser, formulator, and monitor compete for the same attentional pool, so the monitor catches violations after they leave the mouth (or fails to catch them at all). The learner stating the count-noun rule correctly in item 6 of the Present Perfect Continuous quiz while violating it in item 4 of the same quiz is the canonical signature of an unproceduralised rule — *not* a memory failure.

**(c) Why personally meaningful content produced cleaner output (the Past Continuous "I was sleeping when USA attacked Iran" case).** Two mechanisms converge. First, the levels-of-processing framework (Craik & Lockhart, 1972; replicated across cognitive psychology) predicts that semantically deep and self-referentially encoded material produces stronger retention and retrieval than neutrally encoded material. Second, in Kormos's (2006) model, when the conceptualiser already has a pre-formed, emotionally salient message ready, *less* attentional capacity is consumed at the message-planning stage, leaving more for grammatical encoding and monitoring. The practical implication is direct: **practice material must be drawn from the learner's actual life and work** — real Nobitex/fintech experiences, real Kotlin code-review stories, real production incidents — not generic textbook scenarios. This is not a motivational tactic; it is an attention-allocation argument with measurable mechanism.

**(d) Is practice–target mismatch the dominant problem? Yes.** Thirteen sessions of evidence show that more written grammar drilling does not change the production-error curve. Skill-acquisition theory predicts exactly this: practice transfers narrowly to the conditions of practice. With a hard interview deadline, continuing the current method is the single highest-cost decision.

## 2. Evidence-based interventions

### 2.1 For incomplete proceduralisation + 3sg-s / aux-be: structured output + immediate corrective feedback + 4/3/2 task repetition

- **Source.** Lyster & Saito (2010), *Studies in Second Language Acquisition* 32(2): 265–302, meta-analysis of 15 classroom studies, N = 827; oral corrective feedback produced significant overall gains, with *prompts* (output-pushing feedback) outperforming recasts on free-constructed-response measures. Li (2010), *Language Learning* 60: 309–365: overall corrective-feedback d = 0.64, durable in delayed post-tests. Norris & Ortega (2000), *Language Learning* 50: 417–528: focused, explicit L2 instruction produces "large target-oriented gains" (d well above 0.6 across most subcategories), durable. De Jong & Perfetti (2011), *Language Learning* 61(2): 533–568, N = 24 adult ESL: "fluency improved for both groups during training but was maintained on posttests only by the students who repeated their speeches" — the foundational 4/3/2 proceduralisation evidence.
- **Effect size.** d ≈ 0.6–0.7 for corrective feedback on morphosyntax (Li 2010; Lyster & Saito 2010), larger for prompts on free production.
- **Time-to-effect.** Suzuki & DeKeyser (2017), *Language Learning* 67(3): 512–545: distributed practice with a 3.3-day inter-session interval produced small-to-medium effects on oral morphology by Session 3 and gains held at 7- and 28-day post-tests. Detectable improvement within ~3 weeks; durable past the interview window.
- **Efficiency on 1–2 hr/day, 12–16 weeks.** Very high. Replaces declarative drill 1:1 with the practice mode the learner actually needs.
- **Beats the obvious alternative (more written tense drills).** Skill-specificity (DeKeyser & Suzuki, 2025) is decisive: oral-production accuracy comes from oral-production practice. Written drills did not move Pattern 1 in 13 sessions; more sessions will not.
- **Metacognition leverage.** 4/3/2 (Nation, 1989; De Jong & Perfetti, 2011): record monologue 1 (4 min), self-transcribe and tag own -s/aux/preposition errors using already-strong rule knowledge, re-record (3 min), self-tag again, re-record (2 min). The learner *is* his own corrective-feedback engine — high-aptitude metacognition is exactly the profile this works for.

### 2.2 For listening + prosody: shadowing (full-text and selective)

- **Source.** Hamada (2016), *Language Teaching Research* 20(1): 35–52: review of shadowing for listening comprehension. Hamada & Suzuki (2024), *RELC Journal*: deliberate-practice framework for shadowing inside skill-acquisition theory. Whitworth & Rose (2025), *Taylor & Francis*, DOI: 10.1080/29984475.2025.2546827, systematic review of 44 studies: "shadowing training can help improve learners' comprehensibility, intelligibility, and accentedness, as well as certain aspects of suprasegmental pronunciation control, such as fluency and prosody."
- **Effect size.** Tamai's foundational work and Hamada (2016) report significant listening-comprehension gains for lower-intermediate learners after 6 weeks of 10–15 min sessions, 3–4×/week. The 2025 systematic review tempers this with a methodological caveat: most studies rely on controlled-task post-tests; transfer to spontaneous speech is "tentative" but supported.
- **Time-to-effect.** Listening and bottom-up decoding gains visible at 4–6 weeks; pronunciation/prosody gains at 8–12 weeks.
- **Efficiency.** Very high per minute: simultaneously trains aural decoding, prosody, and articulatory motor patterns.
- **Beats the obvious alternative (passive podcast listening).** Shadowing forces alignment of perception and articulation under time pressure; passive listening does not exercise the bottleneck.
- **Metacognition leverage.** IPA-aware self-monitoring (Hamada, 2018, *Journal of Asia TEFL* 15(1)): learner reads the transcript, marks own segmental errors, re-shadows. The metalinguistic skill that powers Part-B accuracy is exactly what makes self-monitored shadowing effective for this learner.

### 2.3 For aural domain-fluency: narrow listening on Android/fintech content

- **Source.** Krashen (1996); Rodrigo & Krashen (2013); Chang & Millett (2017) on narrow reading/listening; Renandya & Krashen (2018, *LEARN Journal*) on extensive viewing. Mechanism: repeated topic-bound input compounds lexical, schematic, and prosodic familiarity, making subsequent input within the topic immediately more comprehensible.
- **Effect size.** Karalık & Merç (2019), *Eurasian Journal of Applied Linguistics* 5(3): 353–383, meta-analysis: vocabulary and grammar knowledge each show **large**-effect-size positive correlations with L2 listening comprehension; metacognitive skills also large; anxiety and self-concept show *large negative* correlations with L2 listening. Narrow listening attacks all three levers (it reduces lexical load, rewards metacognitive prediction, and lowers anxiety).
- **Time-to-effect.** Comprehension gains within 4 weeks of daily narrow-topic listening if the topic stays bounded (one engineer's podcast feed re-listened, not channel-hopping).
- **Efficiency.** Extremely high — aligned with the learner's existing technical reading habit; the schemata already exist.
- **Beats the obvious alternative (Netflix immersion).** Variety-driven extensive viewing scatters lexical reinforcement; narrow listening concentrates it on the actual interview lexicon (microservices, latency, deadlock, build pipeline, idempotency).
- **Metacognition leverage.** Same source re-listened with transcript on second pass; learner marks "spoken-form-of-known-written-word" mismatches — converting his receptive written lexicon into spoken-form recognition, the precise gap behind Pattern 12.

### 2.4 For converting declarative grammar knowledge into noticing during production: dictogloss

- **Source.** Swain & Lapkin (1995); Wajnryb (1990); Qin (2008), *Language Teaching Research* 12; Ishikawa (in Benjamins TBLT volume) on dictogloss + written languaging.
- **Effect size.** Sits in the Norris & Ortega (2000) explicit-instruction band; specifically delivers Swain's "noticing the gap" function — the precise mechanism the learner needs to convert declarative knowledge into self-corrected production.
- **Time-to-effect.** Two to four weeks of 2–3 sessions/week produces measurable noticing gains.
- **Efficiency.** Medium-high — can be done solo with an AI reader (AI reads a short technical paragraph twice; learner reconstructs from notes; compares to the original; languaged self-explanation).
- **Beats the obvious alternative (tense-by-tense written drills).** Dictogloss embeds the form in meaning-bearing context and forces production; written drills isolate the form and skip production.
- **Metacognition leverage.** The reconstruct → compare → languaged-explanation loop is purpose-built for high-explicit-knowledge learners.

### 2.5 For Persian-specific transfer (prepositions, articles, possessor order)

- **Source.** Jalali & Shojaei (2012), *Reading Matrix* 12(1): 80–97, N = 78 Persian-L1 EFL learners, 312 compositions, 642 prepositional errors: **substitution 56%, omission 23%, addition 21%**; the top error category was "wrong selection of correct prepositions, especially those preceding a noun" (20% of all errors); **12 of 15 error categories were fossilised**. Joolaee & Ghonsooly (2015), *Procedia* 192: 438–446: 18.65% (intermediate) / 17.58% (advanced) omission-or-substitution of definite *the* — "deletion of the article poses the greatest problem among the learners and this is because we don't have any equivalent for the definite article *the* in Persian." Ezafe-possessor transfer ("line manager of me") is structurally predicted from Persian grammar (Karimi & Brame; Windfuhr) but under-quantified in journal literature; treat as well-attested descriptively but lacking a single citable error-rate.
- **Effect size.** Fossilised errors are by definition resistant; only explicit, contrastive, repeated practice produces movement (Norris & Ortega, 2000; Spada & Tomita, 2010).
- **Time-to-effect.** Slow. Plan for partial reduction in 12–16 weeks, not elimination.
- **Efficiency.** Treat as a *contained list* — verb+preposition collocations recurring in software-engineering interviews ("work on," "deploy to," "respond to," "report to," "ship to production"). Drill via productive-cloze flashcards and structured output, not via grammar rules.
- **Beats the obvious alternative (preposition rules).** English prepositions are rule-resistant; fossilisation data confirm rules alone fail. Lexicalised chunks are the empirically supported workaround.
- **Metacognition leverage.** Persian–English contrastive pair-cards: explicit L1↔L2 contrast (e.g., "ملحق شدن به X → join X, *not* join *to* X") uses the learner's bilingual analytic capacity.

### 2.6 For fatigue-collapse pattern: spaced, low-stakes substitutes

- **Source.** Heidari (2025), *IRAL*, DOI: 10.1515/iral-2025-0039, N = 92: "spaced practice significantly reduced overall cognitive fatigue, with the effect being most pronounced among low-proficiency learners." Borragán et al. (2017): cognitive fatigue actually *facilitates* certain procedural sequence learning while degrading controlled-task performance — switch from high-control output to high-input/imitation modalities when fatigued. Suzuki & DeKeyser (2017): distributed (3-day) > massed for L2-morphology proceduralisation.
- **Practical rule.** On low-energy nights, substitute *shadowing* and *narrow listening* (input/imitation modalities tolerant of fatigue) for production tasks (which require executive control the learner does not have on those nights). **Substitute, do not skip.**

## 3. Twelve-to-sixteen week protocol

### 3.1 Daily structure (target 90 min; floor 60 min on low-energy nights)

| Block | Technique | Standard (90-min) | Low-energy substitute |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — warm-up (10 min) | Selective shadowing of one 2-min Android/fintech audio clip | Listen → shadow ×2 → silent shadow ×1 | Listen-only narrow listening of the same clip |
| 2 — core output (30 min) | 4/3/2 monologue on a real interview question (e.g., "Walk me through your last production incident") with self-recording | 4 min → self-tag -s/aux/preposition errors → 3 min → self-tag → 2 min final | **Skip output**; replace with 20 min dictogloss on a 3-paragraph engineering blog post |
| 3 — form-focused (20 min) | Dictogloss on a domain text + targeted production-cloze Anki cards | Reconstruct → compare → mark targeted morphemes/prepositions | Same Anki cards, 10 min only |
| 4 — input (20 min) | Narrow listening: one bounded source (Software Engineering Radio, a16z fintech, droidcon talks) | Active listening with transcript on 2nd pass | Same |
| 5 — cooldown (10 min) | Pronunciation/prosody mini-drill: word-stress and primary sentence stress on 5 sentences from the day's audio | Record + compare with model | Skip |

Total weekly load: 5 days of high-control output (4/3/2 + dictogloss + production cards); 2 days of shadowing + narrow listening only.

### 3.2 Weekly progression and difficulty ramp

- **Weeks 1–2 (calibration).** Baseline: record one 3-min monologue on "Tell me about a recent bug you fixed." Transcribe; count -s/aux/preposition errors and disfluencies per 100 words. Shadow at 0.8× speed.
- **Weeks 3–4 (consolidation).** Shadow at 1.0×. Dictogloss 3×/week on technical paragraphs. Begin 4/3/2 with self-recording. Build a personal bank of 30 common interview questions (Android architecture, fintech compliance, system design, behavioral STAR prompts).
- **Weeks 5–6 (load increase).** AI-interviewer mock-technical-deep-dive sessions (whisper-to-text input forces real-time production). Shadow at 1.1× on familiar topics. Counted collocation drills on top 50 verb+preposition combos.
- **Weeks 7–8 (mid-program checkpoint).** Measurement window (see §3.3). Begin live-tutor sessions if possible (italki 2×/week for pushed output with real-time prompt-style recasts — operationalising Lyster & Saito's effective condition).
- **Weeks 9–10 (interview-condition simulation).** Mock interviews on camera, with timer. STAR behavioural answers prepared and 4/3/2-trained. Whiteboard a system-design problem aloud (Android push-notification architecture, payment-reconciliation pipeline) for a 10-min recorded talk.
- **Weeks 11–12 (interview rehearsal).** Full mock-interview circuits with recorded review. Final pronunciation focus on highest-functional-load features for Persian-L1 speakers (word stress on technical compounds — DE-ploy-ment not de-PLOY-ment; vowel reduction in unstressed syllables; /θ/-/ð/ only if a listener flags them).
- **Weeks 13–16 (if available).** Real interview applications. Daily maintenance = 4/3/2 + narrow listening only.

### 3.3 Measurement checkpoints (every 2 weeks)

Record a 3-min monologue on a new interview question and measure:

| Metric | Week 0 baseline | Week 4 target | Week 8 target | Week 12 target | Metric source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Speech rate (syllables/min, pruned) | typically 80–110 at B1 | +10% | +20% | +30% (≥130 syll/min — low-end intelligible-fluent) | Segalowitz (2010); De Jong (2016) |
| Mean length of run (syll between pauses >250 ms) | 4–5 | 6 | 7 | 8+ | Kormos (2006) |
| 3sg-s suppliance in obligatory contexts | TBD | +10 pp | +25 pp | +40 pp (target ≥75%) | Pienemann (1998) |
| Aux-be suppliance | TBD | +15 pp | +35 pp | +50 pp (target ≥85%) | Pienemann (1998) |
| Self-rated comprehension of unfamiliar tech podcast (5-pt) | TBD | +1 | +1.5 | +2 | — |

**Interview-ready pass criteria (Week 12):** speech rate ≥130 syll/min; MLR ≥7; 3sg-s ≥75% in obligatory contexts; aux-be ≥85%; can answer 8/10 random behavioural questions for 90 seconds each without breakdown; can explain one Android architecture diagram for 3 minutes coherently. If by Week 8 the trajectory is below 50% of these targets, escalate (see §5 update triggers).

### 3.4 Where Anki stays and where it retires

- **Retire**: tense-by-tense English grammar drill cards (no evidence they move spoken -s/aux accuracy). Retire generic English-vocabulary cards for words the learner already recognises in writing — his receptive lexicon is intact (Pattern 12); the bottleneck is *spoken-form* retrieval, not meaning recognition.
- **Keep and redesign** as ~150 *productive cloze* cards in three categories:
  - 50 verb+preposition collocations highest-frequency in software-engineering speech, directly attacking the top fossilised category (Jalali & Shojaei, 2012).
  - 50 third-person-singular sentence frames where the learner must produce a full sentence aloud from a prompt and verify against an audio model.
  - 50 fixed multi-word chunks from the Cambridge spoken corpus and software-engineering interview corpora ("I'd argue that…", "the trade-off here is…", "what I'd do differently is…", "off the top of my head…", "let me walk you through…"). Formulaic-sequence research (Pawley & Syder, 1983; Wray, 2002; Tavakoli & Uchihara, 2019, on multi-word sequences and L2 utterance fluency) shows these are stored holistically and free up encoding resources — a direct attack on the attention-allocation problem.
- **Card review budget**: 15 min/day maximum, **with audio production on every card**. Anki without speech reverts to the failure mode.

### 3.5 What to drop entirely

- AI-tutor tense-by-tense written grammar drills.
- Long passive listening to mixed-topic podcasts with no shadowing or transcript pass.
- "Watch more movies" — too lexically diffuse for the deadline (Krashen's narrow-listening rationale).

## 4. Overrated methods for this profile

1. **Continued written grammar drills tense-by-tense.** They train the skill the learner already has (declarative judgement) and not the one he lacks (procedural execution under speech load). DeKeyser & Suzuki (2025) on skill specificity is the direct counter-source; 13 sessions of evidence is the practical confirmation.
2. **Anki vocabulary cards without audio production.** Receptive recognition of an L2 word does not predict ability to retrieve and articulate it in real-time speech; Pattern 12 already demonstrates this. SRS for declarative vocabulary recall is empirically supported (Nakata & Webb, 2016); SRS for *spoken grammatical accuracy* is not.
3. **Generic conversation practice with no error-tagged feedback.** Conversation without corrective input drives fluency *without* accuracy — the learner risks fossilising the exact patterns we are trying to suppress. Lyster & Saito (2010) show output-pushing prompts are what produce accuracy gains.
4. **"Immersion via Netflix."** Variety-rich extensive viewing dilutes the lexical reinforcement needed in 12 weeks for a specific professional domain. Renandya et al. (2018) note that comprehensible-input gains require long timelines incompatible with the interview deadline.
5. **Accent reduction toward a native-speaker target.** Saito & Plonsky (2019), *Language Learning* 69(3): 652–708, meta-analysed 77 pronunciation studies (1982–2017): pronunciation instruction reliably improves comprehensibility and intelligibility but rarely shifts global accentedness toward native norms in adult learners. Target intelligibility (word stress, primary sentence stress, the small set of functional-load segmentals that actually impair comprehension), not native-likeness.
6. **"Speak more, fluency will come" maximalism.** Krashen-style input-only models are empirically contested for production accuracy; Swain's original critique of immersion outcomes still applies. For a 12-week deadline with a measurable accuracy bottleneck, output without focused-form practice will not shift Patterns 1, 2, 4.

## 5. Open questions and update triggers

### 5.1 Genuinely uncertain in the literature

- **Recasts vs. prompts for adults.** Lyster & Saito (2010) show prompts > recasts overall, but the difference is moderated by learner age and instructional setting. For an adult with high metalinguistic awareness, recasts from AI tutors (which supply the corrected form for the learner to compare against his own output) may suffice. Test *both* by mixing self-recasting from learner transcripts with AI-supplied recasts.
- **Whether 3sg-s can be moved meaningfully in 12 weeks.** Pienemann's developmental sequence is robust; the rate of stage-transition under intensive instruction is contested. The teachability hypothesis predicts gains only at the next-emerging stage. The learner *occasionally* produces -s, suggesting he is at emergent Stage 5 — instruction should move it from variable to stable. 100% by Week 12 is not realistic; 75% suppliance is.
- **Effect-size pooling for shadowing.** No full meta-analysis with confidence intervals exists; the Whitworth & Rose (2025) systematic review explicitly notes methodological heterogeneity. Treat shadowing claims as "strong convergent evidence" rather than "established d = X."
- **AI tutor feedback quality.** Empirical literature is thin and rapidly evolving (2024–2026). Treat AI feedback as a complement to, not a substitute for, ≥2 human tutor sessions/week from Week 7.

### 5.2 Update triggers

- **By Week 4:** if speech rate has not increased ≥5%, the daily structured-output time is insufficient or the questions are too lexically demanding. Switch to easier topics for one week, then re-ramp.
- **By Week 8:** if 3sg-s suppliance in obligatory contexts has not moved at least 15 percentage points, escalate from self-recasting to twice-weekly live tutor with strict prompt-style feedback on -s and aux-be exclusively (do not dilute the focus).
- **If fatigue collapse continues despite low-energy substitutes:** move 30 min of the daily allocation to a morning slot. Two short distributed sessions outperform one long session for proceduralisation (Suzuki & DeKeyser, 2017; Heidari, 2025).
- **If interview prompts elicit fluent but error-rich speech at Week 10:** add an "accuracy week" (Weeks 11–12) — push 4/3/2 with explicit -s/aux/preposition focus and accept a temporary fluency dip; complexity-accuracy-fluency trade-off research (Skehan; Kormos) shows this temporary reallocation is normal and recovers.
- **If anxiety/self-concept worsens:** Karalık & Merç (2019) found anxiety has a large negative correlation with L2 listening. Reduce mock-interview frequency for one week and restore narrow-listening confidence-building before resuming pressure.

## 6. خلاصه فارسی (~۳۰۰ کلمه)

**تشخیص اصلی.** مشکل تو حافظه نیست و گرامر ندانستن هم نیست. گرامر را می‌دانی — در بخش تصحیح خطا (Part B) و در توضیح قاعده عملکردت قوی است. مشکل این است که **روش تمرین تو با هدف تو هم‌خوانی ندارد**: تمرین، نوشتاری و آرام و با مانیتورینگ است؛ هدف، گفتاری و بدون مانیتورینگ و زیر فشار زمان مصاحبه است. نظریه‌ی Skill Acquisition (DeKeyser و Suzuki، ۲۰۲۵) می‌گوید مهارت فقط به همان شکلی که تمرین می‌شود خودکار می‌شود. حذف مزمن -s سوم شخص و حذف auxiliary («am»، «be»، «going to») نتیجه‌ی همین است: قاعده در حافظه‌ی declarative هست، اما زیر بار تولید گفتاری زمان واقعی هنوز proceduralize نشده. نظریه‌ی Processability پینمن و داده‌ی محمدخانی و همکاران (۲۰۱۱، روی ۱۵۱ فارسی‌زبان) نشان می‌دهد این دو ساختار از دیرترین مرحله‌اند، پس انتظار نداشته باش در ۱۲ هفته به ۱۰۰٪ برسی — هدف واقع‌بینانه ۷۵–۸۵٪ است.

**سه کار اصلی برای ۱۲–۱۶ هفته.**
۱. **شدوئینگ** روزانه ۱۰–۱۵ دقیقه روی پادکست‌های اندروید/فین‌تک (Hamada، ۲۰۱۶؛ مرور سیستماتیک Whitworth & Rose، ۲۰۲۵). شنیداری و prosody را همزمان می‌سازد.
۲. **روش 4/3/2 Nation** روزانه حدود ۳۰ دقیقه روی سؤال‌های واقعی مصاحبه. یک سؤال را در ۴، بعد ۳، بعد ۲ دقیقه پاسخ بده، خودت ضبط کن، خطاهای -s/aux/preposition را علامت بزن. تو متالنگوئیستی قوی داری — این روش دقیقاً همان توانایی را استفاده می‌کند (De Jong & Perfetti، ۲۰۱۱).
۳. **Narrow listening + Dictogloss** روزانه ۲۰–۳۰ دقیقه روی یک منبع باریک و حرفه‌ای (یک پادکست مهندسی ثابت، نه نتفلیکس).

**دو چیزی که نباید وقت بگذاری.**
۱. **مشق‌های گرامری نوشتاری tense-by-tense را کنار بگذار.** ۱۳ جلسه ثابت کرد جواب نمی‌دهد — همان مهارتی را تمرین می‌کند که داری.
۲. **Anki عمومی واژگان بدون تولید صوتی را کنار بگذار.** فقط ۱۵۰ کارت تخصصی نگه دار: ۵۰ ترکیب فعل+حرف اضافه (Jalali & Shojaei، ۲۰۱۲ — ۵۶٪ خطاهای حرف اضافه فارسی‌زبانان از نوع substitution و ۱۲ از ۱۵ نوع fossilized)، ۵۰ کارت تولیدی -s، ۵۰ chunk چندکلمه‌ای (Wray، ۲۰۰۲). هر کارت با تولید صدا، نه فقط بازشناسی.

شب‌های خسته شدوئینگ و narrow listening انجام بده، نه monologue. خط هدف هفته‌ی ۱۲: speech rate ≥۱۳۰ هجا/دقیقه، MLR ≥۷، -s ≥۷۵٪، توانایی پاسخ ۹۰ ثانیه‌ای به ۸ از ۱۰ سؤال رفتاری بدون فروپاشی.
